Resume:

A moving object projecting its shadow over the water surface induce mosquito larvae to dive, escaping from a potential danger. After repeated occurrences of a shadow, mosquito larvae do not escape any longer due to habituation, the progressive and reversible decrease in response to a reiterative innocuous stimulus. Habituation in mosquito larvae has so far been studied with a bioassay based on dichotomous evaluation of the response (yes/no), which may hide any subtle variation in the responses, when some parameters are manipulated (e.g. rearing conditions, inter-trial interval, stimulus intensity etc.). In this work, we present an original automated system to record behaviour allowing quantitative measurements of the individual response. Fourth-instar Aedes aegypti larvae were individually placed in recipients allowing vertical movements. The experimental setup was illuminated by a white light source and by an infrared light-board placed behind the recipients. In front of the setup, an infrared camera recorded each animal’s movements. Larvae were stimulated with a moving card-board square projecting its shadow (blocking the white light) over the water surface. The shadow induced the escape response in naïve larvae. After 15 presentations of the shadow (training trials), the escape response was significantly lower than in the 1st training trial. A mechanical disturbance stimulus presented after the 15th training trial, induced the escape response as in naïve larvae and reversed habituation in a subsequent presentation of the visual stimulus (test), proving that reduction in response was neither due to effector fatigue nor to sensory adaptation, but to habituation. This method can be used to study the effect of environmental factors (e.g. chemical pollutants) on activity and cognitive abilities in mosquito larvae.
